    👨‍👩‍👧 Family & Patient Education
    Plain language information for parents and caregivers
    ❓ What is extravasation?
    Extravasation happens when a chemotherapy medicine leaks outside the blood vessel during your child's treatment. Instead of going into the vein, the drug seeps into the tissue under the skin. Depending on the drug, this can sometimes cause pain, swelling, or skin changes around the IV site.
    ⚠ Why must the IV site be watched during treatment?
    Some chemotherapy drugs can injure the skin and tissue if they leak outside the vein. The amount of injury depends on the drug type. Your child's nurses and doctors watch the IV site carefully during treatment. Please tell them immediately if your child complains of pain, stinging, or burning near the IV site — do not wait until the end of the infusion.
    🔍 What should parents watch for at home?
    After chemotherapy, check the IV site area daily for: redness that is spreading or getting darker · swelling that is increasing · any change in skin colour (bruising, darkening, purple discolouration) · skin feeling very warm or tender to touch · any blister or open wound forming. If unsure, take a photo each day and show it to the team at the next visit.
    🚨 When should you return to hospital immediately?
    Contact your oncology team or return to hospital immediately if you see: skin turning dark purple or black · an area becoming very painful and not improving · a blister, peeling skin, or open sore at the IV site · fever together with swelling or redness at the IV site · any signs that seem to be getting worse rather than better. Do not wait for the next scheduled appointment.
    💊 Will my child need special treatment?
    It depends on the chemotherapy drug that was given. For some drugs there is a specific antidote medicine. For most situations, cold or warm compresses are applied to the area and the team monitors it closely. Always follow the instructions given by your oncology team — they will tell you exactly what to do at home and when to return.
    📞 Who should we contact?
    Contact your oncology team or the hospital's oncology emergency line as soon as you are worried. There is no such thing as calling too early when it comes to your child's safety. If you cannot reach the team and signs are worsening, go to the nearest emergency department and tell them your child is receiving chemotherapy.
    📚 References & Guideline Sources
    This tool was developed with reference to the following clinical resources. Always verify current versions and adapt to local protocol.
    eviQ Cancer Treatments Online — Extravasation Clinical Procedure
    SLAP approach: Stop, Leave cannula, Aspirate, Photograph/document. Australian standard for anti-cancer drug extravasation management in oncology and haematology settings. Includes agent-specific management tables.
    eviQ Quicklink — Anti-Cancer Therapy Extravasation Summary Table
    Rapid reference table categorising drugs by irritant/vesicant classification, recommended compress, and antidote availability. Used as primary source for drug-specific compress recommendations in this tool.
    ONS/ASCO Chemotherapy Administration Safety Standards (current edition)
    Includes extravasation prevention, recognition, management, and documentation standards. Specifies nurse competency, vascular access considerations, and escalation criteria.
    Pérez Fidalgo JA et al. — ESMO Clinical Practice Guidelines for Extravasation (2012, updated references)
    European guidance on extravasation management including dexrazoxane, DMSO, hyaluronidase, and sodium thiosulfate use. Covers anthracycline, vinca alkaloid, and platinum compound management.
    Dexrazoxane (Totect / Savene) Product Information
    Pharmacist and oncology team verification required for dosing, timing, contraindications, and local availability. First-line antidote for anthracycline extravasation when given within 6 hours of event.
    Local Institutional Extravasation Protocol
    Final management decisions must follow your institution's approved protocol, including drug concentration, line type, patient-specific factors, antidote availability, and surgical pathway.
    Implementation note: Drug classification, compress type, and antidote recommendation in this tool reflect published international guidelines but may differ from local protocols based on available agents, drug concentrations, and institutional policies. Final action always requires verification with the senior oncology team, pharmacist, and where indicated, surgical or plastic surgery specialist. This is a clinical support tool, not a standalone protocol.
